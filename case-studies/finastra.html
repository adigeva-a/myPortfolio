Background & Context

A regulatory mandate with no playbook

The EU's PSD2 directive and UK Open Banking standards mandated that banks expose their payment and account data through secure, standardized APIs by hard regulatory deadlines. Non-compliance meant significant fines. The problem: most banks had no internal API infrastructure, no Open Banking expertise, and no clear path to compliance.

Finastra's opportunity was to build the platform that banks could adopt to meet these mandates â€” turning a regulatory burden into a product. As Senior Product Owner, I led the platform from conception to enterprise bank enablement over four years.

This was a fundamentally different kind of product challenge: the customer was enterprises (banks), the user was third-party providers (TPPs) consuming the APIs, and the regulator was setting the requirements. Balancing all three simultaneously was the central challenge of the role.

Key Learnings

What 4 years of regulated B2B product ownership taught me

  • Regulatory constraints are product requirements â€” not obstacles. The discipline of working within hard constraints produces more creative, focused product decisions than open-ended briefs
  • Enterprise B2B PMs must speak both legal and engineering. The ability to translate between them is the core skill â€” and the rarest one on a product team
  • Developer experience is a product metric on API platforms. Swagger quality and sandbox reliability predicted TPP adoption far better than API feature completeness
  • Requirements traceability isn't overhead â€” it's essential. In regulated products, you need to explain every product decision to auditors and legal teams. Documenting the why at the time it's made saves enormous effort later
  • Pilot programs with real customers find what internal testing can't. Our bank beta participants discovered integration gaps in 3 weeks that 6 months of internal QA had missed
  • Platform metrics differ from consumer metrics. Reliability, adoption velocity, and time-to-activation matter more than individual user experience moments
  • Regulatory ambiguity is a product design opportunity. When the regulator's guidance is unclear, the bank that offers a clear, reasonable interpretation first wins market share
  • B2B PM timelines are dictated by external events (regulatory deadlines, customer contract milestones). Building buffers and being ruthless about scope is survival, not preference
